How Has Men’s Tennis Changed From 1973–2021?

Like elite athletes in other sports, male tennis players are getting taller and heavier every year. The one-handed backhand, once ubiquitous in tennis, is now on the endangered list. There are even some interesting trends on left handers since 1973.

This article analyses all of the above, but before diving in, let's look at how the top countries have shifted over the last 49 years.

Top 6 Countries of Male Tennis Players in the Top 100, 1973–2021

Australia and the USA were once the undisputed leaders in men’s tennis. European heavyweights, Spain and France have made huge gains over the years.

The Decline of the One-Handed Backhand

Racquet technology, strings and slower courts have all contributed to the demise of the one-handed backhand. However, in the upper echelons of the game, this stroke is maintaining a steady 30% of Top 20 male tennis players. Roger Federer is the most well known of the one-handers, but in recent times, players like Dominic Thiem and Stefanos Tsitsipas continue to hold the torch.

Lefty vs Righty

The percentage of southpaws in tennis seems to be remaining steady amongst the Top 100. Things get interesting when we look at Top 20 and Top 10. The proportion of left-handed players falls significantly from the mid-90s onwards. In fact, if it wasn't for one Rafa Nadal, there would be no left handers in the Top 20 for the last decade.

Average Height, Top 100 Men’s Tennis, 1973–2021

Average Weight

Height correlates strongly with weight, however the trend for heavier players seems to have peaked in 2018.

Average Age, Top 100 Men’s Tennis, 1973—2021

Advances in medical science and training have certainly prolonged player's careers. As a result, the average age of the Top 100 has been steadily creeping up. The mid-70s did see many players play well into their late 30s.
